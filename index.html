03 Setup ~4 min

Settings Tour

Standards, tile positions, general settings, sprinkler families, design standards, and the dry fire equivalents. Set up once per project.

  • Before I run Tandm, I'll run you through the settings first. You only need to do this once per project.
  • ShowClick Settings in the Tandm ribbon. Walk through each tab: Standards, Tile Positions, General Settings, Sprinkler Families, Design Standards, then the dry fire equivalents (Smoke Detectors, Speakers).
  • Standards. Tandm ships with the three main standards used worldwide.
    • Australian, the AS standards (AS 2118).
    • US, the NFPA standards.
    • UK, the BS EN standards.
    • You can also work in metric or imperial depending on the project.
  • Tile positions. This is where you tell Tandm which positions on a tile are valid for sprinkler placement.
  • By default we ship with the standard tile positions activated, the centre, mid-points and corners. Most of the time I'd leave them all on, that gives Tandm the most flexibility when it's placing.
  • If for some reason you can't physically place sprinklers in the corners of a square tile, maybe the corners are reserved for lights or air registers in your firm's standard, you can deactivate those positions here. Tandm will respect that and only place where you've told it it's allowed.
  • Custom tile sizes. Same section. If the architect on a specific project is using a non-standard tile size, say a long rectangular tile or a custom dimension, you add it here so Tandm can match the layout to it.
  • General Settings (Soffit Offset for Exposed Areas). This sets how far off the soffit your sprinklers sit in exposed ceiling areas. We've defaulted to the standard maximum, 300mm for combustible and 450mm for non-combustible. You can adjust if you want to lift them to align with your range pipework.
  • Sprinkler Families. We ship with Tandm families and some Victaulic families because of our partnership. I always recommend you use your own families. Load them into Revit like normal, and as long as they're unhosted, they appear in this list ready to map.
  • Design Standards (Custom Hazard Classifications). If you need different spacings for extended coverage, residential, flush, or semi-recessed heads, you create a new class here based on an existing one and edit any field. You can map different families to different classes.
  • ShowCreate a custom hazard class as a demo. "Test light" based on light hazard, change one value, hit Apply. Show it appears in the families list ready to map.

Splitting & Complex Rooms

The most-used tool in Tandm. Break complex rooms into the rectangles the engine handles best, force alignment, handle odd shapes.

  • Tandm's engine works best in rectangles. So for anything that isn't rectangular, like corridors, L-shapes, T-shapes, or rotated rooms, the splitting tool is what you'll use to break the space into shapes Tandm handles well.
  • Think of it the way you'd already break a complex room down in your head as a designer. You probably mentally divide an L-shaped corridor into two rectangles before you place anything. Tandm just makes that explicit.
  • Honestly, this is probably the tool you'll use the most. It's the workhorse of the editing tools.
  • ShowFind a complex room. A corridor with branches off it works well, or an L-shaped office. Show Tandm's first pass placed one big grid that doesn't quite work for the shape.
  • Right, so Tandm has placed a single grid across this whole space. That's the default behaviour, one grid per room. But because the shape isn't rectangular, you can see some sprinklers are flagged orange and the coverage isn't quite right.
  • What I'll do is split this room into rectangular zones. I'll hit the Splitting tool.
  • ShowHit Split. Hold Shift for orthogonal snapping. Draw a split line to break the L-shape or corridor into two rectangles.
  • Notice I'm holding Shift to keep the split orthogonal, that snaps it to 90 degrees. You can also split on any angle if you need to, for an angled or rotated portion of the room.
  • Now I release, and watch what Tandm does.
  • ShowLayout regenerates cleanly within each rectangle. Point out the orange sprinklers from the first pass are now blue.
  • So Tandm has regenerated the layout for each zone individually. Each rectangle now has its own grid, equally spaced apart, equally away from walls within that zone.
  • Now, important thing to notice. The splits are virtual boundaries, not real walls. Tandm is still checking compliance across them.

08 The Magic ~4 min

Orange Walls

Two reasons Tandm flags an orange wall. Multiple ceiling heights (Ceiling Trace), or architect turned room bounding off (Wall Trace).

  • When you see orange walls, Tandm is flagging something that needs your attention. There are two reasons it happens, and Tandm gives you a dedicated tool for each.
  • Reason 1: multiple ceiling heights in one room, or a bulkhead.
  • Tandm's first pass treats the room as flat, so when it detects different ceiling heights within the same room it flags the nib wall between them.
  • Two ways to resolve this. First, manual adjustment with Grid Edit, pushing sprinklers far enough that they spray below the obstruction. Second, Ceiling Trace, which is the one I'd usually use.
  • Ceiling Trace detects the different ceiling zones automatically, splits the room based on that geometry, and recalculates the layout for each zone independently. One click instead of moving every sprinkler manually.
  • ShowFind a room with orange walls and multiple ceiling heights. Toggle ceiling-height visibility so the gradient is visible.
  • ShowRead the gradient out loud: the lighter colour is the highest ceiling, the darker colour is the lowest. A darker shade next to a lighter shade is a step down in the ceiling.
  • ShowDemonstrate Grid Edit option first: push sprinklers away until they turn blue, spraying below the bulkhead. Then revert and demonstrate Ceiling Trace as the cleaner alternative, one click, layout regenerates per zone.
  • Reason 2: the architect has turned room bounding off on certain walls.
  • This is just how some architects model, especially in apartment and residential projects. When Revit creates rooms with room bounding turned off on internal walls, it ignores those walls and creates one big room around the outer perimeter instead of separating the spaces.
  • The data is all there in the model. It's just that Revit's room definition doesn't match what we see visually.
  • Wall Trace lets you bypass the room definition and work straight from the actual wall geometry. Tandm reads the walls directly and rebuilds the layout based on them.
  • ShowFind an apartment area with orange walls but no ceiling step. Click Wall Trace. Watch the walls and layout regenerate together.
  • AskHow often do you come across multiple ceiling heights in a single room, or bulkheads to design around?
  • AskAre you doing much apartment or residential work where architects play games with room bounding?
  • ReinforceEach one of these scenarios used to be a half-hour problem. Now it's two clicks.
  • ReinforceNotice the pattern: Tandm flags what needs your judgement, and gives you the tool to act on it fast.
  • ReinforceThis is the difference between automation that hides problems and automation that surfaces them. Tandm always surfaces.
